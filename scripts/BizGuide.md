## Understanding Global Business

Business Dinner보다는 Business Lunch?

The term “business lunch“ means having a meeting over lunch to discuss business matters. Many business-related meal appointments are scheduled during lunchtime, as it's often the only time when both parties can fit a meeting into their busy schedules. When scheduling a lunch meeting, it's polite to start with a phrase like, “Thank you for including me in your schedule.” While business meals are common in both the United States and South Korea, most business meetings over meals in Korea tend to be more extended and occur during the evening. If you find yourself needing to invite someone to a dinner meeting, it's a good idea to check if it's okay, saying something like, "Is it okay if we take away some family time?" or "I apologize to your family, but is it acceptable?"

## Understanding Global Business

Three rules for cell phone use in business.

1. Unless it is urgent, it is polite to text someone before calling them, so they can decide when it would be convenient to talk. You can text, “I would like to call you, if it is convenient for you to talk now.”
2. Unless it is urgent, you do not have to answer right away. A business response is typically expected within one work day. You can text, “I am sorry but I cannot answer your call right now. I will call you back soon.”
3. Cellphone is a distraction in meetings, so it is better to put your phone in your pocket or bag. Having it on the table is considered distracting as well. If you must take a call, say: “Excuse me but I need to take this call. I will be back shortly.”

## Understanding Global Business

Steve Jobs’ Meeting Rules

Meetings should support work, not take time away from it. Steve Jobs knew how to run short, effective meetings. Here are his three rules to better meetings.

Rule 1: Keep it small
Too many people mean too many voices. Three to five people is the perfect number. Jobs once declined an invitation to a tech meeting by President Obama, because there were too many people.

Rule 2: Keep it short
An effective meeting should not have more than three items on the agenda. That way it is easier to remain focused and get a definite result. It is also important that the three points are clearly connected to the main purpose of the meeting.

Rule 3: Max 30 minutes
According to research, longer meetings are not more productive. People have a short attention span so small 1-to-2-minute chunks of information are easy to digest.

## Understanding Global Business

Asking Questions: East and West

The simple act of asking a question can be interpreted in very different ways by persons from Eastern or Western cultures. In the West, asking questions is regarded as standard practice. In fact, it’s expected of lower-ranking employees to show initiative by asking questions, and even challenge ideas of superiors. It is not perceived as insubordinate or disrespectful, but rather proves that the employee is trying to understand structures and processes better. Of course, context and intent also matter, but in general employers encourage asking questions. In the East, things work differently. Employees may likely feel intimidated by the idea of asking questions. There is a fear that superiors might see questions as threatening, since relationships are important and company structures are much more hierarchical than in the West. Far more emphasis is placed on the importance of politeness and not openly discussing opinions, disagreements, or pointing out flaws.

Discussion: How are questions perceived in your work environment? Are they encouraged or discouraged? In which situations are questions encouraged and in which are they frowned upon?

## Understanding Global Business

### Communication Nuances Between Different Cultures

The challenge of doing business with other cultures include understanding a foreign language as well as cultural rules specific to the other country. Active listening is an important strategy to understand the communication nuances of your international partner.

Key strategies for active listening include; setting a comfortable tone to allow the speaker to express themselves, making eye contact avoiding interrupting the speaker, repeating the speaker’s last words back to them asking questions and being patient.

Here are a few phrases that you can use to show that you are listening:

표현: Please tell me more. {자세히 말해 주세요.}
표현: Go on. {계속 이야기해 보세요.}
표현: I’m listening. {듣고 있어요.}
표현: Let me see if I understand. {제가 이해했는지 확인해 보겠습니다.}
표현: It sounds like … {말하신 내용으로 보면…}
표현: Do you mean … {…라고 말씀하시는 건가요?}

At the end of the day, the most important aspect of active listening is being open to the speaker and demonstrating that you are trying to understand.

## Understanding Global Business

Showing Respect in the West

It is well known in the West that maintaining face is central to the way business and social relationships work in Korea. In Western business culture, showing respect is also important, though the focus is more on the individual.
A polite greeting such as “good morning” or “good afternoon” is a good way to start any interaction. Demonstrating active listening by making eye contact and responding with verbal cues like “I understand” is also a sign that you are taking the person seriously. Professional etiquette, such as addressing someone by their title and using formal language is highly valued. Being open to discussion and feedback is seen as a sign of maturity and respect in the West. Dressing in appropriate business attire is expected in order to show respect for the environment and the people you are meeting.
These expressions and behavior help to create a professional business environment.

In Western business environments, calling people out on their mistakes is pretty normal. In fact, it’s often seen as an important part of developing a strong and effective team. Tackling problems head-on and letting colleagues know about their shortfalls or errors is totally acceptable, though the way you do so may vary between companies.

There is no need to be openly aggressive or even rude, so a polite email will be more than sufficient. A good strategy is to start with a compliment and then suggest areas that need improvement. The following three expressions are good examples:

### Public Holidays in Vietnam

In a business context, the Vietnamese Lunar New Year, known as Tet or Tet Nguyen Dan, is the most significant traditional holiday in Vietnam. It is also the longest public holiday in the country, with up to ten consecutive work-free days.
The primary focus of the Lunar New Year in Vietnam is on old traditions and family time. Visiting parents during the holidays is crucial for most Vietnamese.

Public Holiday - Date
Gregorian Calendar New Year Holiday - January 1
Lunar New Year (Tet) Holidays - February 10
Hung Kings Commemoration Day - The 10th day of the third month of the lunar calendar
Victory Day - April 30
International Labor Day - May 1
National Day - September 2

## Understanding Global Business

### How to Disagree in a Professional Manner

Wherever groups of people congregate, problems are bound to arise. A business environment is no different. The West and East have different strategies for dealing with problems though.
Westerners tend to use the most direct approach to solving problems. Problem-solving is a collective forum where all members contribute.
Easterners have a more articulate way of solving problems. This involves a series of debates, opposing ideas, and brainstorming. Doing so helps to refine strategies and solutions to existing problems.

Here are 5 phrases that could help resolve a problem:
1. Let’s look at it from a different angle and consider another possible interpretation.
2. Let’s focus on how we can encourage communication to ensure that everyone contribute positively to our working environment.
3. Thank you for your comments. I’ll review them with interest.
4. Could you talk me through how you reached that conclusion?
5. I really appreciate you being willing to discuss this.

### Understanding Global Business

Personal Space: East and West

An important cultural difference between the East and West is the concept of personal space. Personal space refers to the physical distance that people maintain from others in different situations. 
In Eastern cultures, such as Korea, personal space is often smaller and more flexible than in Western cultures. In Western cultures, such as the United States, personal space is often larger and more rigid than in Eastern cultures. People tend to stand farther away from each other in public places, and avoid physical contact unless they are very close or intimate.
It is important to be able to communicate when someone is making you uncomfortable,
so here are a few polite phrases to use:
- I hope this doesn’t come as too much of a shock, but ……
- I don’t want to upset you, but ……
- There is something I’ve been meaning to talk to you about ……

### Understanding Global Business

 Different Styles of Thinking

Linear reasoning, is a way of thinking prevalent in Western cultures. It is characterized by a cause-and-effect approach to problem-solving and decision-making. On the other hand, systems reasoning is often associated with Eastern cultures and is characterized by a more holistic, cyclical, and interconnected approach.
Linear thinking is cause-and-effect thinking, where one cause has one effect, and it is effective in solving straightforward problems. The parts are looked at as separate from each other. In contrast, systems thinking addresses problems at their roots rather than providing superficial solutions, focusing on understanding the underlying interrelationships and patterns of change within a system. The focus is on the whole.

Examples of expressions used in linear thinking is:

- ‘If … then’, ‘given that’, ‘therefore’, ‘all else being equal’ and ‘as a result’.
- Expressions used in systems thinking include: ‘Everything considered’, ‘in the end’, ‘after all is said and done’ and ‘holistically speaking’

3 Functions of Small talk

The importance of small talk in business is often highlighted. Here are 3 reasons why:

1. Building Rapport and Trust: Small talk provides an opportunity to make a quick connection with someone,
and demonstrate that you can be trusted.
2. Creating a Positive Environment: Small talk helps to create a positive and comfortable environment for
business interactions.
3. Helping Networking and Relationship Building: Small talk is an essential tool for networking, as it enables
individuals to find common ground, shared interests, and overcome social discomfort.
